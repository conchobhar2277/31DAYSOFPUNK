31 Days of Punk

The punk album for 12/03/2025 is:

Anti Todo

by Eskorbuto

Ben's thoughts

In the late 70s Punk music began to take over the world like a tidal wave, and a product of this rock & roll renaissance was Eskorbuto, a band formed in 1980 nestled in the fishing town of Santurtzi, Basque Country (northern Spain). Eskorbuto is much more than just one of the first exclusively spanish speaking punk bands, they are a cultural legacy that continues to thrive today. At the time of the band's conception, Basque had just become partially autonomous from Spain, which was met with tension by the public. During this time an armed separatist group (ETA) carried out waves of assassinations, bombings, and kidnappings mainly targeting state officials. If the history of punk has taught us anything, it's that this music is intended to be used as a tool to spread awareness of societal injustice, a time just like this. Eskorbuto became the call and the catalyst for a cultural genesis in this region.

In 1985 the band would release their standout album Anti Todo, with frontman Iosu Expósito delivering one of the strongest vocal recordings of the genre. What rips just as much are the strong bass lines, surprisingly funk driven, coming from Juanma Suarez. Pako Galán was a punk drummer if there ever was one, breaking sticks on almost every song. The album opens with “Historia Triste (Sad Story)” which highlights the bleakness of societal decay. Later we hear the only track marked explicit in the whole album “Cerebros Destruidos” which focuses on the sociopolitical conditions at the time leading to government manipulation and the degradation of critical thinking among the nation, which was likely a critique of Basque Country’s heavy anti-terrorism policies that in reality created a police state (The band would later be arrested for their song “E.T.A.”). What I love most about the album are the very final seconds, when Expósito alone whistles the classic American tune “Yankee Doodle,” a song often associated with national freedom.

The music of Eskorbuto is still celebrated today, with countless punk bands covering their songs and even the last original member Pako Galán stepping back into the light to remind us of his legend. Punk music would not have the reach it does without this band, and when you put on Anti Todo and hear the howls of Iosu Expósito, whether you speak Spanish or not, you understand the language.

Connor's thoughts

I don’t believe in music without emotion, because what is one without the other. Music sparks emotion and emotion sparks music.

That’s a cheesy way to open up my thoughts about Anti Todo by Eskorbuto, which as a non-spanish speaker, emotion had a big role in my listening experience. Now, it would be wrong of me to play off Eskorbuto as some run-of-the-mill punk band from the 80s, as they are one of the most influential Spanish bands of all time, blending fast and dirty guitar riffs with loud, jarring lyrics about the political climate in Spain and the isolation of their own members. The album name alone is a testament to what they stood for, which translates in English to “Anti-Everything”.

It’s hard to say what Guitarist Iosu Expósito and Bassist Juanma Suárez would be doing in today’s political climate, with their tragic deaths ending the band’s short run, but during that time, they definitely wanted their message to be heard, and as their trophy, were insulted, censored, and condemned by the state and the law. Very punk rock if you ask me.

Now I think Benjamin does a great job explaining more of the history behind the band and their lyrics, but as far as the album goes, it's a blast of that rebellious British punk of the 70s, but in all honesty, much more angry. Going back to what I mentioned before, I don’t know Spanish, and while I did look up the English translation, it’s plain as day that this band was frustrated, sad, and beaten, looking for a platform to share their anger, to which would be seen by many as a nihilistic outlook on life. The bone-deep emotions expressed by their two dueling vocalists, makes the average listener, such as me, incredibly invested in why the hell they are screaming my ears off. It's fast, beautiful, and enticing, all words that remind me of great punk records.

All in all, Eskorbuto’s “manifesto”, Anti Todo, is a loud way to introduce yourself to one of punk music’s many greats, and the history that rallied a community of listeners who were tired of everything the world had to offer.
