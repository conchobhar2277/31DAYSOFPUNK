31 Days of Punk

The punk album for 12/05/2025 is:

Kick Out the Jams

by MC5

Ben's thoughts

“Brothers, it's time to testify, and I want to know, are you ready to testify? Are you ready? I give you a testimonial: the MC5!”

One of the most essential bands to the formation of punk music, MC5 (Motor City 5), released their live album “Kick Out The Jams” in 1969. It is difficult to convey just how powerful this record is. It is not until you hear the powerful opening speech (quoted above) running straight into their song “Ramblin’ Rose” that you feel your bones shake by the intensity of their performance. You might as well be in that sweaty boiler room yourself. Detroit, known initially for the creation of Motown, birthed this band in the early 60’s, and with the boom of Rob Tyner’s voice, punk was in the motor city. In fact, MC5 to some is considered the first U.S. punk band period, and while that fact is up for debate, what we know for certain is that a lot of our favorite bands would not be the same without MC5. For instance, when I listen to Kick Out The Jams I hear the future sounds of KISS (MC5 would have a direct influence on their hit song “Detroit Rock City”). Bob Seger, the infamous Detroit rocker, was gaining traction at the same time these cats were, and their infectious energy certainly rubbed off on him. One of the biggest names in Rock & Roll, Jack White, would follow in the footsteps of his ancestors when forming the legendary White Stripes. So whether you are familiar with this band or not, you can thank them for their influence.

In the album MC5 honors Detroit blues legend John Lee Hooker with the song “Motor City is Burning” opening the tune by exclaiming “Brothers and sisters, I wanna tell you something - I hear a lot of talk from a lot of honkeys, sitting on a lot of money, saying they're the high society. But if you ask me, this is the high society, this is the high society!” Many bands of this era were warned to not push their political opinions onto their records as it would effectively kill their radio potential and prove bad for business. But in true punk nature, MC5 did not care, they would rather celebrate the real people of the world than put more money into the pockets of millionaires. This group stood as a rallying cry for social change, recognizing that the true problem may be between class rather than between race and gender. In the history of punk, rock & roll, and rioting, they are seen as heroes. And while the final member passed just a few years ago, their legacy still remains as relevant as ever. MC5 IS ALIVE!

Connor's thoughts

It’s the 5th album of the month and you know what time it is? It's time to KICK OUT THE JAMS!!!

Motor City 5 aka MC5 was a band screaming out of Lincoln Park Michigan, formed in the same way lots of great rock bands were, as a high school combo with a lot to say. From those very first few words “are you ready to testify” followed by that crazy rock and roll tone you always knew you loved, it couldn’t be clearer that MC5 was the hammer that carved the title “Detroit Rock City”.

Honestly and truly if I could go back 24 hours and listen to this album again for the first time, I would do it 3 times over. It’s one of those records that just makes you want to move in all directions and replay each track before it even ends. Kick out the Jams is a spectrum of anything and everything you could want in a punk album. It has psychedelic rock about desire, and blues tunes about revolting against mediocrity. It has that 70s before the 70s rock and roll sound “kicking” out of Fred “Sonic” Smith’s guitar, and if you have been keeping up with my thoughts this month, you know I have to highlight those dirty, heavy Micheal Davis bass riffs.

The live album itself is split into two 4-track parts, acting as a great divide between the style and tonal shift in the record. It’s not a crazy shift from their ongoing theme of lust and relationship issues, but it’s something that caught my attention immediately due to the stark difference in lyrics. You go from MC5’s famous “Kick out the Jams’”, a rally cry about kicking off mediocre/bands that jam (Ben would be very sad), in order for MC5 to take the stage, but then after ‘Borderline’ they transition into what I feel is the most important song of the album, ‘Motor City Burning’, a song about the 1967 riots in Detroit fighting against white oppression, and a call for revolution. It’s so subtle initially, yet its lyrics ring out so loud, and it’s only amplified by it noticeably being the slowest song on the recording (Aside from Starship but that’s its own thing). All in all, I loved this album more than anything we have listened to so far, and whether you love punk, hate punk, love rock, or hate rock I promise you just listen through those first 4 tracks and you won’t be able to stop yourself from listening to the last 4.
