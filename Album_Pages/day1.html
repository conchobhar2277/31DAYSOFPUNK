31 Days of Punk

The punk album for 12/01/2025 is:

The Mark, Tom, and Travis Show (The Enema Strikes Back!)

Ben's thoughts

These west coast pop-punk giants found themselves in a burst of popularity around the late 90’s with releases like Cheshire Cat, Dude Ranch, and their smash hit album Enema of the State. And while their angsty, teenage hormone-filled lyrics were apparent from the start, nothing quite matched their stage presence, which could only be described as a punk comedy opera. This live album perfectly encapsulates why Blink-182 was one of the highest selling touring acts of that era. Between their improvised swapping of lyrics, to their inter-song banter (in which they spared no topic), to employing the use of a voice changer so that Satan could join them on stage, critics could bash them all they wanted, but no one reached the modern audience quite like the shameless members of Blink-182. And unfortunately for the prudes, all three permanent members of the band were great musicians. Tom DeLonge was the riff king of the genre, Mark Hoppus could not stop hitting his bass, and Travis Barker is maybe the best known for inventing his own style of drumming. Like them or love them, if you want to hear the real Blink-182, listen to The Mark, Tom, and Travis Show!

Connor's thoughts

Starting off a blog celebrating punk seems weird for a band that advertises themselves as the “Finest Quality Crappy Punk Rock”, but with their mix of immature humor and ludicrously fast guitar, bass, and drums, this album serves as an unofficial compilation of blink’s golden years, and highlights the unique nature of the band that elevated pop-punk from the 90s through the turn of the century. Whether it's the banter between Mark Hoppus and Tom DeLonge, or the lyrics “Take off your pants” being added into not one, but two songs, blink exemplifies what punk is, bringing the feeling of being a rebellious teen in the 90s to the stage, dirty jokes and all.

It's worth mentioning it's not all potty humor and dick jokes, blink takes their unserious dynamic and uses that to write some really good music, and in my opinion it's the silly songs like ‘Family Reunion’ that make serious songs like ‘Adam’s Song’ hit that much harder. To back that up, Mark Hoppus, Travis Barker, and Tom DeLonge show off in these two recorded performances why they are widely considered some of the best in their respective instruments, and as they continued to produce after this, they only got better.

All that said, blink-182 has a special place in my heart. They served as the soundtrack to my high school years, reminding me not to take everything so seriously, and enjoy being a teenager with teenager-problems. Today, as a college student, blink continues to remind me of this.

So whether you love blink, you have never heard of blink, or you just want to laugh at the voice-changer Satan bits they do between songs, you can’t go wrong with giving this album a listen.
